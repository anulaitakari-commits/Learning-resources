What Counts as a Digital Artefact?

A short, grounded answer to a question that comes up a lot, with a simple test you can apply to any borderline case, and searchable examples of what does and does not count.

1The definition

In this module, a digital artefact is a purposefully designed digital learning product: something you build so that learners can engage with content, with each other, or with you, in a way that supports a clear learning goal.

The module frames these as learning products that apply multimodal pedagogy (combining modes such as words, images, sound, and interaction) and embody a pedagogy of care (inclusive, equitable, and supportive of the learner). Podcasts, videocasts, quizzes, and assessment artefacts are the module's own headline examples.

Grounded in the module overview and Unit 2's framing of multimodal pedagogy, multimodal literacy, and the pedagogy of care, drawing on the Open University's Innovating Pedagogy reports and Palloff & Pratt (2013).

2The boundary test

When you are unsure whether something counts, ask these three questions in order. Something is a digital artefact in this module's sense when the answer to all three is yes.

1

Is it digital?

Created, delivered, or experienced through digital technology, whether a web tool, an app, a file, or an online space.

2

Is it designed for learning?

Built with a specific learning purpose in mind, not simply a digital object that happens to exist. It carries or supports learning toward a goal.

3

Does pedagogy drive the design?

The choices behind it are justified by teaching and learning principles. It applies multimodal thinking and cares for the learner's access and inclusion, rather than using technology for its own sake.

Three yeses: it is a digital artefact you can design and justify. A no at step 2 or 3 usually means you have a digital resource or file, not a designed learning artefact. The gap between those two is exactly what this module asks you to close.
